## Today's Update

Today was a closure day at a scale I haven't hit before on this project. Multiple large sequential builds finished simultaneously, two horizontal infrastructure slices reached handoff, and a wide band of type-system and contract work landed across dozens of feature areas. The day wasn't about exploring new territory — it was about executing against plans that were already bounded and getting them to done.

The Platform Access commercial arc is fully closed. The optional-services-and-release sequence (PAR) ran all twelve units: predecessor admission, Enhanced Support entitlement, CustomerOperations fulfillment, the Insurance Offering Interface, the deterministic Synthetic Insurance Adapter with its hard barriers, the full Synthetic Platform Access Sale Mode, optional-service product surfaces, Sale Reliability Contract instrumentation with alerts and dashboard, end-to-end failure and recovery drills, eleven version-bound reviews with conditions and expiry, provider test modes and dogfooding, and finally the invitation cohort release and portfolio close. Platform Access Sale Contracting (PAS) closed its final three units — acquisition and operations workspaces with channel parity, expiry/withdrawal/correction/failure scenario proofs, and the contracting handoff. Platform Access Completion and Commercial Controls (PAC) ran its own twelve-unit sequence end-to-end: version-frozen completion orchestration, completion quarantine and withdrawal stop, the agreement-level Setup Usage Credit ledger, atomic credit reservations and statements, agreement and partition spend admission, spend reservations and scheduled burst allowances, suspicious-usage holds and spend-aware degradation, protected pricing and progressive cadence, payment resolution and commercial disputes, the Sale Receipt surface with Spend Control Center, and finally financial control proof and release handoff. Three completion sequences in one day, each gating on the one before it. The version-bound reviews came before dogfooding deliberately — rehearsing against unreviewed contracts just defers the same discovery to a worse moment.

The Subtenant Acquisition Marketplace (SAM) also completed its full twelve-unit sequence. The financial plumbing here is more layered than anything in the Platform Access arc: listing and acquisition deal aggregates, qualified seller and buyer admission, governed listing and discovery, offers and diligence with immutable closing versions, the Marketplace Funds Flow Interface with adapters, buyer fund securing before transfer, the portfolio transfer service fee, the settlement waterfall and accounting, Program 3 closing orchestration, validation-gated payout and disputes, and the marketplace workspaces with end-user projections. The settlement waterfall is the piece I'm least confident about. The waterfall steps are ordered, the accounting is defined, the adapters are in place — but "ordered and defined" isn't the same as "proven under adversarial input." That gap will surface when this path gets exercised with edge-case fund states, and I'd rather know about it now than after the first real closing runs through it.

Two horizontal infrastructure slices reached handoff today. HS158 (tenant-scoped cache key adoption) started with a classification spike across 802 call sites — inventorying everything before touching anything — then ran three ranked migration batches through `CacheKeyBuilder` with mandatory tenant segments, added cross-tenant cache isolation regression tests for every migrated path, ran a targeted static analysis verification pass, and produced an advisory audit script exposed via a `make cache-key-audit` Makefile target. The risk-ranking before batching mattered: the highest-risk tenant-scoped services went first, which meant the regression tests were written against the most consequential paths rather than the most convenient ones. HS161 (structured logging zero-adoption wave) took the same inventory-first approach — per-file call-site lists with PII flags and count reconciliation across four containers — then ran four targeted conversion batches: 48 CryptoErasure raw `Log::` sites, 54 UserProfile sites across 23 files including the `web.php` route closures, 43 Waitlist sites across 13 files with `WaitlistAdminApiController` leading, and 34 Domain sites across 11 files with the audit channel preserved throughout. The `LogPseudonymizer` for stable email hashing also landed in the logging container — that was a prerequisite for the CryptoErasure batch to be considered actually closed rather than just structurally complete, since raw email addresses in log fields were the specific gap the batch existed to address. Before/after grep verification and the `adoption-metrics` command confirmed real movement.

The DSR Fulfillment Lifecycle Module closed its full sequence — lifecycle and legal invariant inventory, typed transition contract, transactional transition ledger and outbox, request-type fulfillment execution, recovery and deadlines handling, quality gates with lifecycle documentation, transition/isolation/recovery verification, and caller cutover with shallow module deletion. JAS hardening closed eleven units: plan reconciliation against the completed baseline, conflict projection suppression for same-type candidates, idempotent run retries, canonical jurisdiction-bearing Identity evidence requirements, cross-tenant denial matrix expansion, sealed explanation and evidence API payload constraints, RCE adapter constraint to explicit same-tenant Identity links, manual assertion support evidence requirements, no-result projection expiration coverage, and documentation synchronization. The CTA price-change pilot ran from admission boundary discovery through Computable Trust Context contract definition, refusal semantics, boundary-local admission implementation, safe evidence projection, focused tests, quality gates, and the pilot decision publication. Alongside all of that, a significant band of type-system work landed across authentication, evaluation, trust, registry, payment gateway, COPPA, identity, forms, billing, admin, marketplace, MCP, forecasting, and cognitive governance — typed contracts, narrowed assertions, property contract declarations, and PHPStan `mixed` key cast removals. I find this category of work difficult to narrate without either underselling it or making it sound more dramatic than it is. Each individual item is small. Collectively, they close ambiguities that static analysis will now catch before they surface at runtime.

The planning layer closed out with checklist updates, eleven version-bound reviews recorded with conditions and expiry, the platform-access-optional-services-and-release feature archived, the sale order-of-operations master document archived, indexes regenerated after the portfolio closeout merge, architecture decision records updated with new privacy legal corpus entries including the Disney judgment and opt-out propagation material, and auto-generated planning documentation for the completed projects. What all of this enables concretely: the Platform Access commercial arc is settled and archived, future work in that area builds on a closed foundation. The marketplace financial plumbing is defined and sequenced. The two horizontal slices are at handoff with regression coverage, so the next slice in either area starts from a known-good baseline. The type-system work across the codebase means PHPStan catches a class of error that was previously invisible. The open question I'm carrying forward is the SAM settlement waterfall — I have a design I believe in, and I've made a note to revisit it once the first end-to-end closing exercises that path under non-ideal conditions.
