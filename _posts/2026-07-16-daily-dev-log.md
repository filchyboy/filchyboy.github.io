## Today's Update

Today was a closing day in the truest sense — not closing as in finishing small tasks, but closing entire feature tracks that have been open for weeks. Three major feature sets reached completion and were formally qualified for release: the privacy controls DROP/GPC completion track, the subtenant acquisition marketplace, and the viewer-bound retained interfaces system. That's a significant amount of surface area to consolidate in a single session, and what made it tractable was that the individual implementation sequences had already been built out — today was about running the final qualification gates, recording evidence, and confirming the release posture was actually defensible.

The DROP/GPC completion track was the most compliance-sensitive of the three. I started by freezing the privacy-control surface and ownership baseline — that's the document that pins what the system claims to enforce before any gate runs against it. From there I built outward: shared privacy reporting and exclusion contracts, the measurable-visitor and GPC adoption source facts, capability-intersected GPC report and snapshot APIs with proper auth, and the status surfaces rendered in both Privacy Settings and the Customer Evidence View. The aggregate projection for tenant GPC reporting is deterministic — built to produce the same output given the same inputs, which matters for audit scenarios where someone needs to reproduce a historical posture snapshot. DROP got its own full lifecycle: dual-gate activation with admission authority snapshots, vault-backed secret custody for tenant connections, cursor-based intake and reconciliation, the non-bypassable regime-bound outbound exclusion gate, and enforcement wired at every outbound source boundary. I also issued signed aggregate GPC evidence exports and recorded immutable event-time purchase attribution. The synthetic scenarios that validate the end-to-end DROP behavior were the last piece before the release qualification ran. One thing I'm genuinely uncertain about: whether the outbound gate's regime binding is expressed at the right layer. It's currently enforced at the source boundary rather than in a centralized interceptor, which keeps the coupling clear but means each new outbound source has to opt into the enforcement explicitly. That design decision will resurface when future sources are added.

The subtenant acquisition marketplace closed all twelve planned sequence steps. The ordering here was load-bearing: admission (seller/buyer qualification) had to exist before listing and discovery, listing and discovery before negotiation and offer mechanics, offer mechanics before diligence and the immutable closing versions, and all of that before the funds flow interface and adapters. The settlement waterfall and accounting step was where the sequencing got most precise — you can't implement payout and dispute logic until you know exactly what the waterfall produces. I wired the marketplace closing through Program 3, which is the integration point that Program 3 predecessors had to merge admission for (that verification was the first item in the sequence). The marketplace workspaces and end-user projections shipped as the final step. This feature set has moving parts that touch escrow-adjacent patterns — securing buyer funds before transfer, the portfolio transfer service fee, validation-gated payout — and the implementation sequence was designed so that each financial step is guarded by the prior one. Whether that's sufficient for future operator confidence or whether additional reconciliation hooks are needed is a question I've deferred to the operational testing phase.

The viewer-bound retained interfaces system was the largest single implementation sequence by item count, covering everything from the initial contract inventory through final release evidence. The core concept is that analytical surfaces and workbench interfaces are retained per-viewer — meaning they're compiled once, stored with immutable revisions, and served with freshness posture rather than recomputed on demand. Getting that right required defining the Viewer Execution Context, the Interface Request Candidate contract, and the retained definition compiler contract before writing any persistence logic. I added two migrations (definition lifecycle with current-version tracking, and per-viewer invocation outcomes), implemented live and synthetic Shopify evidence adapters with source substitution composition, extended agent responses with typed interface candidates, and added an explicit candidate confirmation endpoint. The Workbench composer and result zone are built, L0 summary/metric-card and bounded table profiles are compiled, and the promotion pipeline handles personal, tenant, and platform owner-routed promotion with explainable signals. One decision I made that's worth documenting: the synthetic Shopify adapter performs substitution at the source level rather than after compilation, which keeps the compiler's provenance chain clean. The alternative would have been to inject synthetic data post-compile, but that would have broken the immutability guarantee on stored revisions.

Outside the main feature tracks: I addressed review findings for both DROP/GPC and the retained interfaces surface, corrected the composer landmark hierarchy (a real accessibility regression that needed fixing before the verification record could be written), refreshed the admin UI continuation guide, and established five-package market readiness in the GTM planning layer. The VPC consent authority provider integration got its P0 security items resolved — unevidenced authority trust elevation is contained, client evidence receipt is no longer treated as verified VPC, and consent method labels are aligned. Those were the right things to fix before the P1 work (ADR, versioned requirements decisions, provider installation persistence, consent grant authority binding) could be defined on solid ground. The five-package GTM readiness work is interesting in that it forced an honest accounting of what's actually complete versus what's sequenced — the answer is that the core platform is further along than the GTM surface suggests, and closing the gap there is the next real priority.
