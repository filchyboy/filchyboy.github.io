## Today's Update

Today was a consolidation and closure day on a scale I haven't had in a while — not because the individual pieces were particularly complex, but because the breadth of what came together was wide. Six thin vertical slices reached quality gates and handoff, five horizontal slices closed out completely, and a cluster of architectural foundation work that's been accumulating got properly documented and archived. The through-line across all of it is that most of today's work reduces ambiguity for future implementation paths rather than adding new surface area.

The six vertical slices — global search with Meilisearch activation (tv517), self-service billing visibility (tv519), publication public-view telemetry (tv521), authorization denial observability (tv524), marketing budget live tab (tv525), and license inventory SBOM ingest (tv526) — each ran the full sequence from contract scope baseline through backend orchestration, data determinism, API contract hardening, frontend integration, observability instrumentation, focused tests, and quality gates. Running six in parallel is a deliberate tradeoff: the sequences are mechanical enough that context-switching between them isn't expensive, but it does mean each one gets less deep attention at any individual step. The one I'm least confident about is tv524 (authorization denial observability). The observability instrumentation step produces spans and metric increments on denial events, but I haven't verified that the signal is discriminating enough to be useful — meaning it may count denials without enough contextual labels to distinguish a misconfigured tenant from a genuine policy violation. That's a gap I'll need to address before this path is exercised at any volume.

The five horizontal slices that closed — hs146 (queue job failure handling), hs147 (dispatch after-commit consistency), hs148 (Eloquent strict-mode lazy-load guardrail), hs150 (migration guard closure wave 2), and hs151 (trace context propagation through jobs and console) — each represent a different category of platform hygiene that's now documented and tested rather than inferred. The hs147 work is worth calling out specifically: remediating in-transaction dispatch sites requires understanding blast radius before moving anything, so the work was sequenced spike → batch 1 (idempotency, governance, publication, case management, campaign) → batch 2 (remaining cluster) rather than just sweeping alphabetically through the codebase. The hs150 migration guard work was the most mechanical of the five — three batches of guard additions across ~293 previously unguarded migrations, plus an exemption registry — but "mechanical" doesn't mean unimportant. Every unguarded migration is a double-run hazard, and having an explicit exemption registry makes the coverage auditable rather than approximate.

The runtime evidence contract work (twelve steps from container configuration through targeted quality gates) and the artifact provenance attribution feature (fourteen steps from planning normalization through quality gate evidence) both reached completion today. These two are architecturally adjacent: the runtime contract produces signed, atomic artifacts that describe what actually ran, while the provenance attribution system tracks distribution intent, sealing, and signature profiles for content. Together they form most of what's needed to make a verifiable claim about what happened to a piece of content from generation to delivery. The commercial policy settlement layer also closed — the authorization decision schema, tenant-scoped idempotency action, billing trace lineage, Context Briefing cutover paths, and negative cross-tenant tests are all in. The hs153 metrics-to-alerts activation, the computable trust architecture documentation, and the architecture policy audit DSR trace remediation round out what closed today on the observability and policy side.

The honest accounting: closing this many things in one day means some of the quality gate evidence is thinner than I'd like. The focused tests across the horizontal slices were targeted rather than exhaustive — by design, since exhaustive tests at this stage would be testing against contracts that haven't been validated by real usage patterns yet. The more pressing question is whether the hs149 FormRequest tenant-base adoption wave 1 (89 files reparented across Commerce, Admin, and the small-container cohorts, with 297 Core residuals catalogued for wave 2) actually caught all the places where raw `FormRequest` was doing implicit authorization through validation rather than through a policy gate. The classification spike covered 432 classes, but classification isn't the same as verification. That's where the regression tests — specifically the 401/403/422 contract tests per cohort — need to do the real work.
