## Today's Update

Today's work fell into two main threads that don't have obvious overlap, but both share the same underlying logic: close the gaps between what the platform claims to support and what's actually wired up, tested, and defensible before any of this runs under real load.

The larger thread was the Product Management Workbench — 47 work units from first principles to a complete, tested feature. I started by settling a question that's easy to leave ambiguous: confirming `AggregatedProduct` as the canonical workbench product boundary, documenting what that means in terms that survive the next round of implementation, and inventorying which routes already existed versus which ones needed to be added. From there the sequence was additive — new admin route, Commerce navigation entry, React entrypoint, the workbench shell, then pulling in `UnifiedProductList` and `UnifiedProductDetail`. The list surface got summary counters, an extended filter contract, advanced search, sortable table headers with accessible markup, and per-row actions. The detail surface was reorganized around a cleaner tab taxonomy: content and variants, read-only inventory, pricing, sources and mappings, sync and conflicts, related orders, audit projection, and surface usage metadata. Conflict and duplicate actions got scoped to the product level rather than being global operations. Create and edit flows came after the read surface was stable. All of that runs against synthetic storefront fixtures — defined, seeded, covering products, pricing, inventory, conflicts, and duplicates — so the workbench has something real-shaped to render against. Backend and frontend quality gates ran, evidence was recorded, and three guides landed: admin usage, unified product API, and synthetic data. The honest note here is that the tab taxonomy decision is one I'm not completely settled on — there's a reasonable argument that sync-and-conflicts and sources-and-mappings belong together rather than as siblings, and I may revisit that before this surface is exercised by future tenant operators building pricing workflows on top of it.

The second thread was the backend/UI feature gap audit — a deliberate pass to classify every place where a backend capability exists without a mounted admin surface, or where a surface exists without properly scoped contracts. I validated the audit evidence first, then defined the admin capability manifest and mounted-route parity coverage so there's a machine-checkable record of what's supposed to be mounted where. From there I worked through three specific surfaces in sequence: Analytical Telemetry (contract, API implementation, admin page mount, accessibility verification), Collision Engine (tenant-context contract, live admin routes, surface verification), and Operational Twin (tenant route contract, page mount without scope escalation, route/auth/accessibility verification). The scope-escalation constraint on the Operational Twin surface is worth naming explicitly — the tenant admin page needs to render operational twin data without acquiring platform-level read permissions, which required the contract to define exactly what the tenant-scoped projection exposes. Getting that wrong at the contract stage would have meant debugging authorization failures at runtime rather than catching the boundary problem during design. The audit closed with reusable guidance promoted out of the planning artifacts and a clean planning loop closure.

Alongside those two threads, there was a cluster of deferred work that reached resolution. The deep-security runs (including Run 6 residual HIGH remediation verification and capturing Run 12 and Run 14 validation evidence) are closed. The identity governance registry's live policy enforcement is now wired into TrustFabric MCP and TOON enforcement rather than sitting as a pending connection. The ATP interoperability spikes — external attestation import adapter, portable envelope mapping layer, selective disclosure proof-of-concept, and partner pilot readiness gate — converted from open research into defined outcomes. Security-audit stub implementations were hardened, platform stub-mode gaps were resolved, and staging branch protection landed. There's also a `ListCalendarWaitlistRequest` form request for validating calendar waitlist filters, which is a small standalone piece but completes the input validation coverage for that surface.

The gap audit and the workbench together mean the admin layer now has a documented, tested, and route-verified surface across analytical telemetry, collision engine, operational twin, and product management — all scoped correctly. The synthetic data fixtures give the product workbench something to exercise before real tenant catalog data exists. The next logical move is to bring the customer operations and planning documentation work that the tracker picked up today into a similar audit cycle, but that's a separate thread for a separate day.
