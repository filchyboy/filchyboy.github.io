## Today's Update

Today was about building the architecture I should have built months ago. I finally tackled the trust management mess that's been haunting me - trust decisions scattered across containers, inconsistent classification schemes, and no coherent view of what agents can actually do with sensitive data.

The breakthrough came when I extended the TrustTier enum from a simple three-tier model to a proper five-tier taxonomy with INTERNAL and UNKNOWN levels. This sounds boring, but it unlocked everything else. I rebuilt the TrustTierNormalizer with proper alias handling, updated the SourceTrustClassifier to work with the new model, and created a PolicyEngine service that can actually make intelligent decisions about agent operations. The best part was integrating this with the ToolExecutionPolicy preflight checks - now agents declare their trust requirements upfront instead of us guessing what they're allowed to do.

I also built out the entire TrustFabric container from scratch, complete with ActionProposal and PolicyDecision DTOs, a ManifestBuilder service for well-known endpoints, and a PolicyDecisionRecord model to track every trust decision the system makes. The escalation response handling was trickier than expected - I had to think through what happens when an agent requests something that needs human review, how long those reviews stay valid, and how to surface the decision history in the frontend. The PolicyDecisionList and TrustPostureSummary React components turned out cleaner than I hoped.

On the compliance side, I completed the entire PolicyGovernance container architecture - migrations for jurisdictions, policy types, clauses, and versions, plus all the supporting models and services. The PolicyCompilerService can now take regulatory requirements and generate versioned policy documents with proper provenance tracking. I built out the publishing API, approval workflows, and audit logging. The integration tests revealed some edge cases around clause dependency resolution that I hadn't considered, but those are working now.

The agent-egress work surprised me with how much infrastructure it needed. I thought it would be a simple policy check, but proper egress control requires data classification, policy evaluation, request brokering, redaction services, and comprehensive audit logging. I ended up with 39 separate components, from the EgressIntent DTO to the InfisicalAgentVaultDriver to the tenant isolation tests. The Redactor service alone needed careful thinking about what constitutes sensitive data and how to handle it consistently across different output formats.

All this architectural work sets me up to tackle the agentic design system properly. I've got trust levels, policy enforcement, and egress controls in place - now I can build agent interfaces that actually respect security boundaries instead of hoping for the best.
