## Today's Update

Today was a convergence day — several multi-week threads closed simultaneously, which is unusual. Most days have one or two feature sets reaching completion; today had closer to a dozen, across layers that don't normally finish in the same window.

The three platform-access programs all landed their final units today. The Platform Access Sale Contracting sequence (pas-01 through pas-12) completed the full arc from verifying merged foundation interfaces through qualification fingerprinting, authority ceremonies, immutable terms assembly, jurisdictional projections, checkout/payment/tax integration, contract snapshot rendering, post-authorization revalidation, and finally the acquisition and operations workspaces with channel parity. That last step — the workspace build — is the surface future tenant operators will actually use when conducting a sale; everything before it was the machinery underneath. Similarly, Platform Access Completion and Commercial Controls (pac-01 through pac-12) ran the full sequence from completion orchestration through credit reservations, spend admission, anomaly degradation, protected pricing, payment failure resolution, and the Sale Receipt surface. The Optional Services and Release program (par-01 through par-12) closed out insurance offering interfaces, deterministic synthetic adapters, full synthetic sale mode, CustomerOperations fulfillment, reliability contract instrumentation, and the recovery drills. Finishing all three programs in the same day is a coordination artifact more than a planning achievement — the dependency chains happened to align. What it means practically is that the publish packets for all three are now ready: sale contracting interfaces, optional services interfaces, and completion/commercial interfaces each have their evidence packets recorded.

The Subtenant Acquisition Marketplace (sam-01 through sam-12) also finished today, which is the piece I'm most uncertain about despite it being formally complete. The sequence covers listing and acquisition deal aggregates, qualified seller and buyer admission, governed listing and discovery, offers and diligence with immutable closing versions, the Marketplace Funds Flow Interface and adapters, buyer fund security before transfer, the portfolio transfer service fee, settlement waterfall and accounting, marketplace closing through Program 3, validation-gated payout and disputes, and finally the production workspaces. That's a lot of ground, and the settlement waterfall in particular is the step I'd want to revisit before this path handles real money. The accounting logic is specified and tested, but settlement waterfalls tend to surface edge cases that aren't visible until the first anomalous deal structure hits them.

Two horizontal slices and the Jurisdiction Attribution Service hardening also closed. HS158 — tenant-scoped cache key adoption — ran from the initial inventory spike (802 call sites catalogued) through three migration batches, an advisory audit script with a `make cache-key-audit` target, cross-tenant isolation regression tests, and quality gates. The residual cohort is named, which matters: "residual" in this context means the sites that weren't migrated in the batch sequence have an explicit record rather than silently remaining on unscoped keys. HS161 — structured logging zero-adoption wave — converted 179 raw `Log::` sites across four containers: 48 CryptoErasure sites, 54 UserProfile sites across 23 files including the web.php route closures, 43 Waitlist sites, and 34 Domain sites with the audit channel preserved. The PII flags from the inventory spike shaped which batches ran first and in what order, which is exactly the point of doing the inventory before touching any code. The JAS hardening closed 11 items: idempotent retry semantics, conflict projection suppression for same-type candidates, canonical jurisdiction-bearing Identity evidence requirements, cross-tenant denial coverage, sealed explanation and evidence API payloads, RCE adapter boundary constraints to explicit same-tenant Identity links, manual assertion support evidence requirements, no-result projection expiration behavior, and documentation sync. The RCE adapter constraint is the one I'd call load-bearing — without an explicit same-tenant Identity link requirement, the attribution logic has a boundary that's easy to violate accidentally.

The DSR Fulfillment Lifecycle Module (dflm) landed as a self-contained unit: lifecycle and legal invariant inventory, typed transition contract, transactional transition ledger with outbox, request-type fulfillment execution, recovery and deadlines, caller cutover with shallow module deletion, and transition isolation tests. The outbox pattern here is the right call for a data subject request system — you need the transition ledger write and the downstream event to succeed or fail together, and an in-process callback doesn't give you that guarantee. The docs hierarchy audit remediation thread also closed, having worked through frontmatter workflow commands, link-validation policy, MkDocs nav missing-target ledger, design-system navigation retargeting, high-confidence link repairs, user-facing placeholder replacement, directory index coverage improvements, and manual backlog triage. The CTA price-change pilot wrapped its full sequence from boundary discovery through context contract, refusal semantics, boundary-local admission implementation, evidence projection, focused tests, and the pilot decision publication. Whether the pilot decision is the right call is something I'll know more about once the admission logic runs against real price-change candidates rather than synthetic fixtures.
