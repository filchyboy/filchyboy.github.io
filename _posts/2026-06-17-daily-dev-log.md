## Today's Update

Two large feature areas dominated the day: the content provenance system (CPDCP) and the super-admin synthetic data tool. Both reached meaningful completion thresholds, which is why today felt more like closing chapters than accumulating work-in-progress.

The content provenance work went from empty directory to a functioning evaluation pipeline. Seven migrations establish the schema: provenance records, attributions, regulatory disclosure rules, takedown notices, disclosure evaluations, regulatory regimes, and tenant disclosure policies. That's a deliberately normalized structure — I kept regulatory regimes in their own table because a single tenant policy will eventually reference multiple jurisdiction-specific rule sets, and collapsing that into the policy table would make rule inheritance awkward. From the schema I built the models (`ContentProvenanceRecord`, `ContentProvenanceAttribution`, `RegulatoryRegime`, `RegulatoryRule`, and their companions), then implemented the three core tasks: `UpsertProvenanceRecordTask`, `RecordAttributionTask`, and `QueryProvenanceRecordsTask`. The `DisclosureEvaluatorTask` is where the logic actually lives — it consumes the regulatory regime and rule models through a `RegulatoryDisclosureRuleService` and produces a deterministic pass/fail decision with supporting reasons. I wrote tests against the evaluator directly, which was the right call here: the decision logic is the riskiest surface, and testing it in isolation means I'm not relying on integration fixtures to catch a wrong evaluation branch. I'm not completely certain the current rule priority model handles conflicting regime requirements correctly when a tenant operates under more than one regulatory regime simultaneously — that edge will surface when I extend the regime coverage, but it's documented as a known gap rather than something that slipped through.

The synthetic data tool is the more unusual piece of work to describe, because it spans every layer of the stack. On the backend: `is_synthetic` columns land on `forms_form_definitions`, `event_definitions`, `publication_posts`, `publication_channels`, and `publication_feeds` through five separate migrations. Each domain got its own seeder living under its Porto container path — `SyntheticFormsSeeder` under `Core/Forms/Data/SyntheticData`, `SyntheticEventsSeeder` under `Core/Events/Data/SyntheticData`, `SyntheticPostsSeeder` under `AppSection/Publication/Data/SyntheticData`, and `SyntheticPublicationsSeeder` covering channels and feeds. The `BulkSyntheticDataController` exposes two endpoints — `stats` and `bulkGenerate` — both protected by an environment gate that refuses execution outside non-production environments. I also audited `SyntheticStreamController` for gate consistency while I was in that area, because inconsistent env guards are the kind of thing that only matters once and then matters a lot. On the frontend, `syntheticDataAdminApi` is a typed service layer that the React panels consume; `SyntheticDataManagement` is the shell panel with lazy export, and the three section components (Stats, Populate, Remove) are self-contained. The Remove section uses a typed-confirm modal, which adds friction appropriate to a destructive bulk operation. The Stats section surfaces the last 20 entries from `DemoModeAuditLog`, which I extended to record bulk-generate operations — so there's an actual audit trail rather than relying on server logs to reconstruct what ran and when.

Beyond those two, several smaller items closed out: the consent management admin dashboard got accessibility and functionality improvements, redirect handling now preserves the origin URL across authentication flows (important to get right early — this kind of state threading is miserable to retrofit), the admin CRM controls were standardized, dropdown theme handling was aligned, product surface form contracts were integrated, and Playwright version references were updated alongside relevant testing documentation. The security documentation for run 14 was revised with full re-investigation results. None of these are dramatic, but the redirect handling change is worth calling out specifically — preserving origin through auth redirects means future tenant workflows that deep-link into authenticated surfaces won't silently drop users at the dashboard root after login.

Tomorrow the content provenance system needs its action layer — the tasks exist, but the orchestrating actions that tie attribution recording to disclosure evaluation haven't been written. That's the next concrete step before CPDCP is wirable into actual content publication flows.
