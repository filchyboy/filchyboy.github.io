## Today's Update

July 6 was the kind of day where the breadth of work is almost misleading — the sheer count of completed items obscures what was actually happening, which was a sustained push to close out a large cluster of infrastructure work that had been accumulating across multiple horizontal slices simultaneously. Fourteen horizontal slices (hs132 through hs145) all reached their handoff states today, plus the policy obligation traceability audit, the regulatory traceability infrastructure, twelve thin vertical slices, and a handful of cross-cutting concerns. That's a lot of concurrent closure, and it happened because most of these tracks had been progressing in parallel and converged at the quality-gate stage at roughly the same time.

The most architecturally significant work today was the regulatory traceability infrastructure — 67 items spanning schema contracts, classification evaluation, and trace propagation across multiple source domains. The core of this was building a `PolicyRequirementRegistry` with canonical ID minting and an admission action, then wiring `RegulatoryReviewOverride` records with proper idempotency contracts so that repeated override patterns surface as amendment candidates rather than silently accumulating. The classification evaluator is declarative: given a `MachineReadableRegulatoryPolicy` fixture, an applicability condition evaluator, and a risk profile resolver, it produces an output hash that covers rule, risk, evidence, and scope together — meaning a classification result is pinned to its full evaluation context, not just its conclusion. That determinism matters for audit: when a future regulator asks "why was this classified this way on this date," the hash is the receipt. The trace propagation work extended that through `TrustFabric` policy decision records, governance receipts, DSR evidence, consent evidence, and billing regulatory evidence, each domain now carrying admitted `PolicyRequirement` trace refs in its output. I'm less certain about the masked evidence layer — the `MaskedRegulatoryEvidenceReference` contract and the privileged raw-evidence reveal authorization feel right in principle, but I haven't tested the reveal flow against a genuinely adversarial access pattern yet. That's a gap I want to revisit before this path gets exercised in any meaningful way.

The compliance horizontal slices tell a more sequential story. HS133 (DSR data collection scope) delivered a real pipeline: `DataCategoryScopeRegistry` codifying what categories exist, `DataCollectionService` executing tenant-scoped ACCESS and PORTABILITY collection, export file generation persisted to storage, and a deletion-scope enumerator driving per-category deletion strategies. Both `ProcessDataExportRequestJob` and `ProcessDataDeletionRequestJob` are now wired to actual logic rather than stubs. HS134 added the `DataCategoryDeletionMapping` model and migration, a deletion target resolver, a scheduled retention enforcement job that produces deletion proofs, permission-gated legal-hold endpoints, and a mounted legal-hold admin surface. HS136 tackled tenant isolation at the job layer — classifying which jobs are tenant-scoped versus platform-scoped, then wrapping the highest-risk cohort in `TenantAwareJob` in wave 1 and the remainder in wave 2, plus a ship-level platform-scope wrapper that replaces bare `withoutGlobalScopes` calls in hotspot services. The cross-tenant denial tests for that work are mandatory and they passed, which is the only signal I actually trust for isolation correctness. HS135 converged all webhook idempotency through a shared enforcement seam with Prometheus metrics pairing, then rolled five Email webhook processors and the Comms delivery processors onto that seam, with duplicate-suppression telemetry and regression tests per webhook family.

Twelve thin vertical slices (505 through 516) all completed their full sequence today — contract scope baseline, backend orchestration, data determinism, API contract hardening, frontend integration, observability instrumentation, focused tests and accessibility, and quality gates — covering everything from unsubscribe preference compliance (TV512) and subscription lifecycle admin (TV506) through CSV import batch status (TV516) and reinforcement intelligence console (TV514). The structured pattern across all twelve means the work is consistent, but I'll be honest: the agent surfaces registry activation slice (TV515) and the relational memory live wiring slice (TV513) are the ones I'm least confident are fully battle-hardened at the backend orchestration layer. They completed, but the "data determinism" step for anything involving relational memory wiring is doing real work that I want a second pass on before treating it as settled. The frontend LOC remediation also closed today — the 1001+ LOC files are reduced and the legacy `max-lines` baseline is removed, which means future violations will be caught at commit time rather than accumulating until someone notices.

The planning record work at end of day archived hs132 through hs145 plans, recorded completions for hs135, hs138, hs139, hs140, hs141, hs143, hs144, and hs145, archived the July 6 reconciliation, and normalized tracker statuses across the board. That's less interesting to write about than the infrastructure work, but a planning system that's out of sync with what's actually been built is a liability — future planning sessions start from the wrong baseline, and that compounds. The archive pass matters. Tomorrow the operational workbench architecture and operations center routing registry work have concrete planning artifacts; those are the next things waiting.
