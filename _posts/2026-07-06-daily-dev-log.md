## Today's Update

July 6 was the heaviest single-day output I've had on this build — somewhere north of 300 work units across 34 feature areas. Rather than try to narrate every thread, I want to focus on what the day actually represented architecturally, because there's a coherent story underneath the volume.

The largest single body of work was the Policy Regulatory Traceability Infrastructure, which absorbed 67 units and is the thing I'm most uncertain about in terms of "did I get the design right." The core problem it solves: when the platform makes a compliance-relevant decision — a TrustFabric policy decision, a DSR access grant, a consent record — there needs to be a traceable chain from that runtime event back to the specific regulatory requirement that justified it. I built this out as a layered system: a machine-readable regulatory policy schema (with typed data objects, a `RegulatoryControlIntent` enum, and an applicability condition evaluator), a `PolicyRequirementRegistry` with canonical ID minting and an admission action, and a `RegulatoryReviewOverride` mechanism that's authorization-scoped by ownership role and flags repeated override patterns as amendment candidates. The version-pinned replay contract and the `RegulatoryTraceProjectionAdapter` sit on top of that — the adapter's job is to render trace references into governance receipts and evidence exports without inventing context or over-disclosing raw identifiers. I'm genuinely unsure whether the masked evidence reference layer is the right abstraction boundary. It felt necessary to separate "what the export shows" from "what the database holds," but that separation adds a deserialization step on every evidence read path that I'll want to benchmark before this becomes a high-volume path.

The 14 horizontal slices (HS132 through HS145) each reached full closure today, which is a different kind of accomplishment — less about any single design decision and more about a systematic push to complete work that had been partially done. HS133 (DSR data collection scope enumeration) is a good representative: it went from a personal-data source inventory through a `DataCategoryScopeRegistry`, a tenant-scoped `DataCollectionService`, wiring of both `ProcessDataExportRequestJob` and `ProcessDataDeletionRequestJob` into real pipelines, real export file generation persisted to storage, and cross-tenant isolation tests that would catch any accidental data bleed between tenants. HS136 (tenant isolation guardrail wave) is the one I'd mark as highest risk-reduction value: classifying every model and job as tenant-scoped vs. platform-scoped, applying `BelongsToAccount` to email models, wrapping the highest-risk jobs in `TenantAwareJob` in two waves, and replacing bare `withoutGlobalScopes` calls in hotspot services with a ship-level platform-scope wrapper. Before this, those isolation boundaries existed by convention. Now they exist as enforced contracts with cross-tenant denial tests. The compliance mutation audit work (HS137) produced an immutable `ComplianceMutationAudit` model with a `RecordsComplianceMutations` observer hook capturing actor and diff — every inventoried compliance entity now gets that hook attached, and the mutation stream is wired into the SOC2/GDPR evidence export. That's the kind of thing that's essentially free to add during a greenfield build and extremely expensive to retrofit.

Twelve thin vertical slices (TV505–TV516) each completed their full loop today: contract scope baseline, backend orchestration, data determinism, API contract hardening, frontend integration, observability instrumentation, focused tests with accessibility, and quality gates. The slices cover a range of product surfaces — unsubscribe preference compliance, subscription lifecycle admin, agent surface promotion, campaign analytics, CSV import batch status, permission admin audit, auth funnel telemetry, refund dispute triage, and a few others. The uniformity of the loop structure is intentional; each slice is supposed to be independently deployable and fully observable. What's less uniform is the underlying complexity — the relational memory live-wiring loop (TV513) and the reinforcement intelligence console (TV514) involved more backend surface area than something like the CSV import batch status loop. The frontend LOC remediation followup also closed out: the 1001+ LOC source files are reduced and the legacy `max-lines` baseline is removed, which means the lint gate is now enforced without an escape hatch.

The policy obligation traceability audit (12 units) and the daily brief reconciliation work are worth naming briefly because they're the planning infrastructure that makes everything else coherent. The audit produced a full authority-first Policy Requirement catalog, a trace chain matrix, a test coverage matrix, and a gap analysis with remediation waves. That's not glamorous, but without it the regulatory traceability infrastructure built today would have been built against implicit assumptions rather than explicit requirements. The operations center routing registry metadata and the operational workbench architecture planning also landed — those feed directly into the next architectural phase, which is making the admin surfaces navigable as a unified operator console rather than a collection of independently-mounted pages.
