## Today's Update

Today had three distinct workstreams running in parallel, and the interesting thing about the day is that they don't share obvious surface area — but they all share the same underlying constraint: getting contracts and quality guarantees locked before the next layer of work can proceed.

The two thin vertical slices — TV-564 (CRM Contact Push Sync Activation Loop) and TV-563 (MCP FinOps Dashboard Production Mount Loop) — both ran full eight-step implementation sequences today, from contract freeze through quality gates and handoff. The structure of these slices is deliberate: freeze the journey contract first, then backend orchestration, then deterministic tenant-scoped data, then API contract hardening, then frontend integration, then observability instrumentation, then quality gates, then handoff. The ordering isn't arbitrary. If the journey contract isn't frozen before orchestration starts, the orchestration ends up encoding assumptions that later harden into implicit contracts nobody explicitly agreed to. What I've found doing enough of these slices is that the "freeze the contract" step is the one people want to skip when schedule pressure exists, and it's also the one that creates the most rework when skipped. TV-563 is now archived. TV-564 is in the same state. Both slices have instrumented observability signals and verified accessibility — future operators exercising these paths won't be guessing at what's happening inside.

The ESLint ratchet remediation work was the heaviest single effort of the day, and it's the kind of work that's easy to mischaracterize. This wasn't a one-pass lint fix — it was nineteen discrete units covering meaningfully different problem shapes. The Tiptap command-chain type issues in `RichTextEditor` required actual diagnosis before the fix was clear: the command chains had lost type safety somewhere in an upstream dependency version boundary, and the resolution was restoring explicit types rather than suppressing the errors. The `PriceChangeManager` prop documentation, the `PolicyCenter` view extraction and handler stabilization, and the wind-down eligibility derivation naming and memoization all fall into the category of "the component was doing too much in one place and the lint rules were the messenger."
