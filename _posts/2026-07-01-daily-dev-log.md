## Today's Update

July 1st ended up being one of those days where the scope only becomes clear in retrospect. Four major threads ran in parallel — the Operational Intelligence Workspace, the Tiered Cognitive Retrieval Layer, Synthetic Data Expansion, and the Forecasting cross-links — plus a cluster of planning, documentation, and dependency maintenance work that tends to accumulate between larger feature pushes. By the time I was reconciling the July review gaps in the evening, it was obvious this had been less of a "focused sprint" day and more of a multi-front advance that happened to land simultaneously.

The heaviest lift was the Operational Intelligence Workspace, which I built from schema through frontend in a single pass. The design work came first: confirming boundaries and source contracts, then designing the projection schema that anchors the whole thing. Three migrations followed — `operational_observations`, `operational_evidence_references`, and `oiw_user_state` — and I ran the migration identifier lint after each round rather than saving it for the end. That order matters because migration naming collisions in Porto are easier to catch before the models exist; once you've written an Eloquent model against a bad identifier, you're refactoring two things instead of one. The source adapter interface established what all three adapters — Operational Intervention, Decision Authority, and Context Briefing — are expected to implement, and the adapters followed in that sequence. From there: the projection refresh action, the attention query task, the evidence graph task, and the history query task. The API layer came next (requests, resources, controller, routes), and then the React modules: Attention Center, Investigation Detail, Evidence Graph, and the history module, all styled with CSS Modules and registered in the frontend module catalog. Tests ran against each layer — source adapters, projection refresh, query tasks, API endpoints, frontend service, component behavior, and accessibility. Both backend and frontend quality gates passed before I finalized the release handoff and added the admin web entry point.

The Tiered Cognitive Retrieval Layer was architecturally more speculative than the OIW work, and I want to be honest about where I'm less certain. The GVRL contract baseline came first to establish what already existed, then I added the TCRL-specific domain contracts on top. Three new schemas: vector representation state, embedding regeneration recipe, and retrieval budget policy. The models, factories, and registry tasks built against those schemas, followed by the effective placement service, adapter routing through representation state, and budget enforcement. The regeneration workflow has a source contract defined, but the source owner discovery spike I ran afterward surfaced a real open question — several candidate regeneration owners exist across container boundaries, and the right authority model for triggering regeneration isn't resolved. I've documented that as an explicit follow-on rather than letting it drift. The Portable Retrieval Recipe export, Knowledge State Evidence projection, metering posture hooks, and retrieval manifest explanation evidence all landed before the quality gates and canonical documentation updates closed the track. The cross-tenant aggregate retrieval authority spike is genuinely uncertain territory; parent-child aggregate semantics at the retrieval layer have implications I'm not ready to commit to schema.

The Synthetic Data Expansion work was different in character — more about making Demo Mode operationally trustworthy than about building new features. I defined the tranche activation standards first, because without those the seeder tests have no contract to assert against. The Product Management synthetic tranche got the full treatment: markers on `ProductAggregation`, a dedicated seeder, model key registration, CLI path, and tests covering seeding, stats, purge, and workbench reads. The legacy `ProductManagementSyntheticStorefrontSeeder` bridge decision got made and committed. Beyond Product Management, I hardened independent inactive registered tranches and repaired the security event / threat detection tranche relationship, where the linked-data dependency wasn't being respected during seeding. The preset order stats consistency work activated the tranches that had proven stable, and four follow-on plans landed for the domains not yet covered: PriceCatalogue, CDP and Communications, Bookings and Calendar, and the governance/agent/accessibility/feature-flag cluster. The Forecasting OIOF cross-link track was comparatively contained — source recommendation lookup, tenant isolation tests, frontend service and types, the linked posture UI in Forecasting recommendations, tests, docs, and a deliberate deferral of the Forecasting-owned status handoff decision. That deferral was intentional; I don't want OIW and Forecasting sharing status ownership before the OIW handoff stabilizes.

The July 1 brief work, planning archives, and governance duplicate-delivery prevention were the connective tissue. The duplicate delivery guard in `governance-receipts` is one of those additions that's invisible when it works and painful when it's absent — future event pipelines will generate enough volume that idempotency enforcement at the receipt layer earns its keep before the first real tenant ever runs a workflow. Five dependency bumps also landed across CodeQL, Trivy, setup-php, and actions/checkout, keeping the CI surface current. The OIW plan, the Synthetic Data Expansion plan, and the TCRL plan are all archived now. What's ahead is primarily the regeneration source owner question in TCRL, the remaining synthetic data tranches, and whatever surfaces from the clean read of the OIW release before anything external touches it.
